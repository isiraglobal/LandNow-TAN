Investment Thesis

The structural case for land and water.

Published June 2026

The aquifer is the account.

The western United States sits on infrastructure built a century ago for a climate that no longer exists. Allocations were set when annual rainfall was reliably higher. Every major basin — the Colorado, the Rio Grande, the Ogallala — is structurally overallocated. This is not a cyclical drought. It is a permanent reduction in supply meeting permanently rising demand.

Municipal water districts need firm supply. They cannot issue a bond against a speculative allocation. So they buy senior water rights — and the price of those rights has compounded at rates that would make most venture portfolios envious.

Institutional capital is coming.

Pension funds, endowments, and family offices are increasingly allocating to natural capital. Land is the only hard asset that also produces cash flow — whether through agriculture, leases, or optionality on future development. The problem is that most institutional buyers lack the on-the-ground infrastructure to source, underwrite, and manage these assets.

That gap is where operators win. The people who can walk a ditch, read a title chain, and value water rights independently of the land they sit under will be the ones who control the next cycle.

The inefficiency is the edge.

Agricultural land trades in a market that is decades behind institutional real estate. Sellers are fragmented. Information is localized. Financing structures are bespoke. The same characteristics that make this market difficult for large capital make it perfect for networked operators.

LandNow exists to collapse that informational advantage into a single network of people who share intelligence, co-invest, and close.
