The Landscape

The structural case for land and water has never been stronger.

The western United States operates on infrastructure built a century ago for a climate that no longer exists. Every major basin—the Colorado, the Rio Grande, the Ogallala—is structurally overallocated. This is not a cyclical drought. It is a permanent reduction in supply meeting permanently rising demand.

Municipal water districts cannot issue bonds against speculative allocations. So they buy senior water rights. The price of those rights has compounded at rates that would make most venture portfolios envious. The institutions have noticed. Pension funds, endowments, and family offices are increasingly allocating to natural capital—and they are competing for a finite pool of assets.

Land is the only hard asset that produces cash flow. Whether through agriculture, leases, or optionality on future development, the underlying asset generates while it appreciates. The problem is that most capital lacks the on-the-ground infrastructure to source, underwrite, and manage these assets at scale.

That gap is where this network operates. The professionals who can walk a ditch, read a title chain, and value water rights independently of the land they sit under will control the next cycle. LandNow exists to collapse the informational advantage into a single network of people who share intelligence, co-invest, and close.
